What Size Generator Do I Need for My House?

Real wattage numbers for homes of every size ‚Äî and why the answer almost never requires powering everything at once.

Key insight: "Whole-house" generator sizing is almost always overkill for most homeowners. The better question is: what do I actually need to run during an outage? A 2,000‚Äì3,500 sq ft home usually needs 5,000‚Äì10,000W for essential loads ‚Äî not the 20,000W a true whole-house generator requires.

Two Ways to Size a Generator

Before diving into numbers, you need to choose your sizing philosophy:

Option A: Essential Load Coverage

You size for only what you actually need during a power outage: refrigerator, freezer, some lighting, a phone charger, possibly a window AC or space heater, and critical systems like a sump pump or well pump. This approach typically requires 5,000‚Äì10,000W and costs $800‚Äì$5,000 for the generator plus transfer switch.

Option B: Whole-Home Coverage

You size to run everything in the home simultaneously ‚Äî including central HVAC, electric clothes dryer, electric range, and EV charger. This requires 15,000‚Äì25,000W and a permanently installed standby generator costing $8,000‚Äì$20,000+ installed. Most homeowners who pay for this rarely use it to full capacity.

Our recommendation for most homeowners: Essential load sizing. It's 70‚Äì80% less expensive, covers everything you actually care about during an outage, and is far easier to execute. Use our Generator Size Calculator to determine your specific essential load.

What Adds the Most Watts?

These appliances will dramatically increase your generator requirement if included:

  • Central air conditioner (2.5‚Äì5 ton): 3,500‚Äì6,000W running, 7,500‚Äì15,000W startup. The single biggest load driver.
  • Electric water heater: 4,500W running. Consider delaying water heating or using propane during outages.
  • Well pump (1 HP): 1,000W running, 3,000‚Äì4,000W startup.
  • Electric clothes dryer: 5,000‚Äì6,000W. Rarely a genuine emergency need.
  • Electric range/oven: 5,000‚Äì12,000W. A propane camp stove is a practical substitute during outages.
  • EV charger (Level 2): 7,200‚Äì9,600W ‚Äî largest load in modern homes without proper load management.
Practical tip: For electric heating, central AC, and EV charging, a portable generator is rarely a cost-effective solution. Standby generators sized for whole-home loads are the appropriate technology for those use cases.
